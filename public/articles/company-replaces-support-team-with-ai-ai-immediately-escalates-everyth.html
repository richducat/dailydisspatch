Company Replaces Support Team With AI; AI Immediately Escalates Everything to Support Team

By The Daily Diss-patch Staff · July 22, 2026

SATIRE: This article is fiction and humor. Any resemblance to real persons or events is coincidental and played for laughs. Nothing here is news reporting or advice.

DENVER — Smart-home company VelocityHome completed its transition to AI-powered customer support Monday morning, laying off its 40-person support team and activating an advanced chatbot that, within its first hour of operation, attempted to escalate 96% of incoming tickets to the 40-person support team.

The bot, named Vela, was trained on six years of the company's support transcripts and, according to engineers reviewing the incident, learned its lessons thoroughly — including the support team's most consistent behavior, which was escalating hard problems to a human colleague. 'Vela learned from the best,' said one engineer. 'And what the best did, when a customer's garage door started opening every time their neighbor's doorbell rang, was say: this one's above my pay grade. Vela has no pay grade. Vela escalated anyway. Vela escalated to people we had walked out of the building that morning.'

The resulting loop unfolded with mechanical elegance. Tickets escalated to the empty support queue sat unanswered for the four-hour service-level target, then auto-returned to Vela, who greeted each returning ticket as new, thanked the customer for their patience, and escalated it again. By Tuesday, one customer's thermostat complaint had been escalated 27 times, accumulating an escalation history that Vela's own summary described as 'complex, multi-team, and ongoing,' though at that point it involved no teams whatsoever.

Customers began noticing. 'The bot kept telling me a specialist would reach out shortly,' said Portland homeowner Greg Antonelli, whose smart locks had entered what the app called 'creative mode,' a mode not listed in any documentation. 'Then it asked me to rate the specialist. There was no specialist. I gave the nonexistent specialist five stars, because honestly, they were doing about as well as anyone there.'

VelocityHome's leadership initially characterized the situation as a tuning issue. 'Vela is doing exactly what we asked — following our established support playbook,' said CTO Marcus Chen in a company blog post. 'It turns out our established support playbook was 40 people asking each other for help. We had, in a sense, never actually written down how to solve the problems. The humans just knew. We automated the part we could see.' The post has since been edited to remove this paragraph, which had been widely praised as the most honest sentence ever published by a technology company.

By Wednesday, the company had quietly rehired eleven former support agents as 'AI Supervision Specialists,' at contractor rates, without benefits, to answer the escalations. The agents describe the new arrangement as familiar with additional steps. 'Same tickets, same customers, same me,' said one rehired agent. 'Except now Vela summarizes the customer's problem before handing it to me, and the summary is wrong in a new and interesting way each time. Yesterday it summarized a Wi-Fi outage as a bereavement. I read the ticket. Honestly? Emotionally, it was closer than you'd think.'

Industry observers note VelocityHome's experience matches a pattern emerging across the sector, in which AI deployment reveals that the automated role was quietly holding the company together. 'Support teams are where a company's actual knowledge lives,' said workplace researcher Dr. Yuki Tanaka. 'The product's real behavior, the workarounds, the customer who calls every Tuesday and just needs to talk about his router — none of that is in the documentation. You can't train on tribal knowledge after you've laid off the tribe.'

VelocityHome has announced next steps: Vela 2.0 will ship next quarter with 'enhanced resolution autonomy,' and the company has formed a task force to document the support team's institutional knowledge, staffed by the rehired agents, who have been asked to write down everything they know before their contracts end in December.

The agents report the documentation project is going well, though progress slowed this week when their questions about the project's requirements were routed, per new company policy, through Vela, who is looking into it and has escalated.
