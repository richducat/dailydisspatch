HOA Newsletter Declares War on 'Unauthorized Joy'

By The Daily Diss-patch Staff · July 22, 2026

SATIRE: This article is fiction and humor. Any resemblance to real persons or events is coincidental and played for laughs. Nothing here is news reporting or advice.

PELICAN CREST, FL — The October newsletter of the Pelican Crest Homeowners Association arrived in mailboxes this week, and residents describe its 14 pages as 'a masterpiece of the genre' — the genre being documents that begin with the phrase 'a friendly reminder' and escalate, by page four, into something scholars of the form call 'total war.'

The newsletter's centerpiece is a new enforcement initiative targeting what the board terms 'unauthorized joy,' defined in Appendix C as 'any expression of delight, festivity, or whimsy not pre-approved via Form JY-1 (Notice of Intent to Celebrate), submitted no fewer than ten business days in advance.'

Violations cited in the current issue include: wind chimes 'exceeding the community's established tone palette,' a garden gnome described as 'confrontational,' seasonal decorations displayed 'out of season, in season, or in a manner anticipating a future season,' and an 8-year-old's birthday party that 'exceeded its permitted scope' when guests were heard laughing after 4 p.m., a time the bylaws designate as Reflection Hours.

Board president Sandra Voss-Whitley, now in her eleventh consecutive term following elections she describes as 'increasingly ceremonial,' defended the initiative. 'People move to Pelican Crest for standards,' she said from her home office, where the community's 214 houses are represented by pins on a large map, several ringed in red thread. 'Nobody is against joy. We are against unregulated joy. There is a process. When joy goes through the process, it comes out the other side as approved merriment, and everyone can enjoy it with confidence.'

The newsletter also includes the association's beloved recurring features: the Compliance Corner, which this month celebrates a resident who reported her own hedge; a two-page essay by Voss-Whitley titled 'Beige: A Love Letter'; and the Violations Map, which residents study 'like a war table,' according to homeowner Terry Okafor. 'You look for your house first. Then you look for your enemies' houses. It's the only part of the newsletter everyone reads. It's the sports section.'

Resistance is quietly building. A group calling itself the Pelican Crest Liberation Front has begun meeting in a garage — an unapproved use of a garage, per Section 12 — to plan what members describe as 'acts of coordinated delight.' Their first operation, planned for spring, involves every household simultaneously installing one (1) wind chime, on the theory that the board cannot cite everyone. Veterans of the community are skeptical. 'They cited everyone in 2019,' one longtime resident warned. 'The Mailbox Uprising. We do not speak of how that ended. But the mailboxes are all the same now. You've seen them.'

Legal experts note the association's authority is, remarkably, mostly real. 'People sign these covenants without reading them,' said property law professor Dan Reyes. 'Page 40 of the Pelican Crest CC&Rs grants the board authority over paint, fences, pets, flags, and, I am quoting directly, ambiance. They have jurisdiction over ambiance. I show this document to my students the way medical schools show rare diseases.'

Voss-Whitley dismissed talk of rebellion as 'the same energy we see every autumn,' and noted that the board has planned a community event to boost morale: the annual Fall Festival, scheduled for a Tuesday, 2 to 3:30 p.m., music at conversational volume, fun concluding promptly at 3:30 whether complete or not.

Attendance, the newsletter notes, is mandatory. Joy at the event, it clarifies in bold, is not.
