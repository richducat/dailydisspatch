Area Landlord Raises Rent to Cover Rising Cost of Raising Rent

By The Daily Diss-patch Staff · July 21, 2026

SATIRE: This article is fiction and humor. Any resemblance to real persons or events is coincidental and played for laughs. Nothing here is news reporting or advice.

MELBOURNE, FL — Tenants of the Sandpiper Court apartment complex received letters this week announcing an 11% rent increase, which landlord Gary Plotnick explained is necessary to offset the mounting expenses associated with raising rent.

'People think this is easy,' Plotnick said, gesturing at a laptop displaying RentMaxx Pro, a $400-a-month pricing platform that recommends optimal increases. 'The software subscription. The legal templates for the increase letters. The certified mail. The hours I spend fielding angry calls about the increase — my time isn't free. All of that costs money, and unfortunately, those costs get passed on. I don't make the rules. I subscribe to the rules.'

The letter itself, obtained by the Diss-patch, is a masterwork of the genre. It opens with 'We value you as a resident,' pivots immediately to 'market conditions beyond our control,' cites 'rising operational costs' without enumerating any, and closes with an invitation to renew early to 'lock in' the new, higher rate, described as a 'loyalty opportunity.'

Tenants have questions. 'Last year it was inflation,' said Dana Okonkwo, a nurse who has lived at Sandpiper Court for six years. 'The year before it was property taxes. This year it's, what, the cost of increasing it? What's next year — a surcharge for the emotional labor of cashing my check? The pool has been 'opening soon' since 2023. The soon is load-bearing at this point.'

Plotnick disputes the characterization that nothing has improved, pointing to several recent investments: the parking lot was restriped, the lobby received what he describes as 'a gallery wall' (two prints of sailboats), and the complex was renamed from Sandpiper Court to 'The Sandpiper Residences,' a rebrand he says 'repositioned the asset' and cost $12,000, which is in the rent now too.

Economists confirm Plotnick's approach reflects national trends. 'We're seeing rent increases justified by increasingly abstract inputs,' said housing researcher Dr. Felicia Marsh. 'First it was costs. Then it was projected costs. Now we've reached costs incurred by the increase itself — a rent ouroboros. The logical endpoint is a landlord raising rent to cover the therapy he needs from raising rent, and honestly, I give that eighteen months.'

The RentMaxx Pro software at the center of the increase has its own perspective, in a sense. The platform's marketing materials promise landlords it will 'remove the emotion from pricing,' which Plotnick confirmed it has. 'I used to feel bad,' he said. 'Now the dashboard just shows a green arrow. It's hard to feel bad about a green arrow. That's worth the $400 right there. Which, again, is in the rent.'

Tenants organizing a response face the usual obstacles. A building group chat formed within hours of the letters, generated 400 messages of unified outrage, and by evening had fractured into a dispute about whether someone's window air conditioner was the real problem. Plotnick, informed of the chat, was unbothered. 'They did the same thing last year,' he said. 'The chat is part of the cycle. Frankly, the chat does more for retention than the gallery wall. It gives them community. You can't put a price on that.' He paused. 'Although.'

At press time, Plotnick was reviewing a RentMaxx Pro notification recommending a mid-lease 'market adjustment,' a feature that costs an additional $99 a month, which he described, without apparent irony, as 'getting expensive.'
