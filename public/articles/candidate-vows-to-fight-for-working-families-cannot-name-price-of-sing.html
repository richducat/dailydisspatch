Candidate Vows to Fight for Working Families, Cannot Name Price of Single Grocery Item

By The Daily Diss-patch Staff · July 22, 2026

SATIRE: This article is fiction and humor. Any resemblance to real persons or events is coincidental and played for laughs. Nothing here is news reporting or advice.

CEDAR FALLS, IA — Senator Chip Halloway's campaign swing through the heartland hit turbulence Tuesday when the three-term incumbent, moments after declaring himself 'the last line of defense for working families,' was unable to estimate the price of milk, eggs, bread, or, in an improvised lightning round he requested himself, 'any item sold in any store.'

The exchange occurred at Verna's Diner, a mandatory stop on the campaign trail where candidates traditionally roll up their sleeves, order black coffee, and absorb authenticity from the booths. Halloway had successfully completed the sleeve portion when a local reporter asked what a gallon of milk costs.

'Milk,' Halloway repeated, buying time with a technique aides call 'the thoughtful nod.' 'You know, milk prices are exactly why I'm fighting. Families are hurting.' Pressed for a number, the senator's eyes performed what witnesses describe as a search of the middle distance before he answered: 'I'd say somewhere in the American range. Nine dollars? Is it nine? It's not nine.' It was not nine.

The senator's attempts at recovery followed the classic sequence. First came the folksy deflection ('My wife handles the shopping, and thank goodness, because she drives a hard bargain'). Then the pivot to biography ('I grew up stacking hay bales, and let me tell you, we knew the price of things then'). Finally, the counterattack: 'The real question is why Washington insiders are obsessed with gotcha grocery questions instead of solutions.' Halloway has served in Washington for 19 years.

His campaign moved quickly to contain the damage, releasing a statement that evening asserting the senator 'has always been laser-focused on kitchen-table issues' alongside a photo of Halloway at a kitchen table. Internet users noted the table was staged in a model home, the refrigerator visible behind him was still wrapped in delivery plastic, and the fruit bowl contained what appeared to be decorative wax pears.

Verna Kowalczyk, the diner's owner of 34 years, has seen this before. 'They all come through,' she said, wiping the counter. 'They order the coffee, they call it the best coffee in the state, they leave a twenty like it's a grand gesture. In 2016 one of them asked me if the eggs were locally sourced. Sir, this is Iowa. You cannot get away from the eggs. The eggs are sourced from across the street.'

Voters interviewed at the diner expressed less outrage than weary recognition. 'I don't need him to know the exact price,' said retired machinist Dale Prusak. 'I need him to know it went up. Ballpark it, that's all. Say six bucks and change and move on. The wax pears, though.' Prusak shook his head slowly. 'The wax pears are what I'll remember in the booth.'

Political scientists note the grocery test has felled candidates for decades, yet campaigns remain unprepared. 'Every cycle, someone's staff briefs them on missile systems and trade policy and forgets the milk,' said Dr. Carol Jennings of Drake University. 'The milk question is the entire job interview. It asks: do you live in the same economy as the people you represent? There are only two answers, and one of them is nine dollars.'

Halloway's opponent, for her part, seized the moment by filming a campaign ad inside a grocery store, correctly pricing eleven consecutive items before an aide gently stopped her from continuing down the aisle. Her campaign has since released the footage under the title 'She Knows,' which polling suggests is, at this point, the entire bar.
