Bank Introduces $12 Fee for Explaining Fees

By The Daily Diss-patch Staff · July 21, 2026

SATIRE: This article is fiction and humor. Any resemblance to real persons or events is coincidental and played for laughs. Nothing here is news reporting or advice.

CHARLOTTE — Meridian Trust Bank announced a new $12 'Clarity Charge' this week, applied whenever a customer requests an explanation of a fee on their account, in a move the bank describes as 'aligning the cost of transparency with those who consume it.'

The fee, disclosed on page 34 of an updated account agreement whose delivery email was itself flagged by spam filters, applies to fee-related inquiries made by phone, in branch, or through the bank's chat assistant. Asking about the Clarity Charge triggers the Clarity Charge, a recursion the bank acknowledges and describes as 'working as intended.'

'Explanations aren't free to produce,' said Meridian Trust spokesperson Alan Drummond, explaining for free under the traditional press exemption. 'When a customer asks why they were charged, a representative must locate the fee, read its name aloud, and stand by while the customer reacts. That's labor. Our research shows the average fee explanation takes four minutes. We simply ask customers to share in that cost, at a rate of three dollars per minute, which is competitive with tutoring.'

The Clarity Charge joins Meridian's existing portfolio of maintenance fees, paper statement fees, digital statement fees, inactivity fees, excessive activity fees, and a $6 monthly 'Relationship Fee' that customers receive regardless of whether they consider the relationship to be going well. Banking analysts note the new fee's innovation lies not in its size but its position. 'It's a fee that guards the other fees,' said consumer finance researcher Dr. Priya Natarajan. 'A moat around the castle of fees. Historically, the customer's only power was asking questions. Meridian has priced the question. It's genuinely new. I had to explain it to my colleagues three times, and under Meridian's framework, I'd have billed $36.'

Customers discovered the fee in the wild almost immediately. 'I saw a $12 charge I didn't recognize, so I called to ask what it was,' said account holder Teresa Okafor. 'The rep said it was the Clarity Charge from when I called last month to ask about a different charge. I said I never called last month. She checked and agreed — the previous fee was a mistake. So they refunded that one, but the new $12 stood, because I had, quote, consumed an explanation. I asked her to explain what she just said. She got very quiet. She knew what the silence meant. We both did. That silence cost me twelve dollars.'

The bank's chat assistant, Meri, has been updated to support the new policy and now responds to fee questions with a consent screen reading 'Understanding costs $12. Proceed?' Early data shows 40% of customers proceed, 30% abandon the chat, and 30% attempt to trick Meri into explaining for free by phrasing the question hypothetically. Meri has been trained on hypotheticals. Meri charges for hypotheticals.

Consumer advocates have filed complaints, arguing the fee effectively charges customers for exercising their right to understand their own account. Meridian's legal team responded that customers who prefer not to pay can consult the fee schedule themselves, a 60-page document whose table of contents notes, in a line item advocates have called 'either an oversight or a masterpiece,' that printed copies are available for $12.

Rival banks are watching closely. One competitor has already announced it will never charge customers for explanations, in a press release its marketing team titled 'Clarity Is Free Here,' sent to customers as a push notification, the settings for which can be adjusted for a small fee.

Reached for final comment, Drummond emphasized that the Clarity Charge can be waived for premium account holders, students, and any customer who asks for a waiver, though he noted, after a pause that suggested he was hearing the sentence for the first time as he said it, that asking for the waiver is an inquiry, and inquiries are $12.
