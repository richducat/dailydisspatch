Company Returns to Office to Boost Collaboration; Employees Now Collaborate on Finding New Jobs

By The Daily Diss-patch Staff · July 21, 2026

SATIRE: This article is fiction and humor. Any resemblance to real persons or events is coincidental and played for laughs. Nothing here is news reporting or advice.

AUSTIN — Six weeks after software firm Meridian Dynamics mandated a full five-day return to office to 'restore the magic of in-person collaboration,' executives are celebrating a measurable surge in teamwork, though a closer look at the data suggests the teams are working on something else entirely.

'Collaboration is up 340%,' Chief People Officer Denise Harmon announced at an all-hands, citing badge swipes, conference room bookings, and 'hallway energy.' The figures are accurate. Employees confirm they have never collaborated so intensely, primarily on shared spreadsheets titled things like 'Q3 Escape Plan' and a company-wide referral network that has so far placed eleven colleagues at competitors, four of which are fully remote.

'Honestly, the office has been great for networking,' said senior engineer Marcus Webb, speaking from a phone booth pod he had booked under the name 'Sprint Planning.' 'Before, if I wanted a referral to another company, I had to message someone on LinkedIn like an animal. Now I just walk to the third floor. Tuesdays are when the recruiters' emails go out, so Tuesday lunch is basically a job fair with free pizza. The pizza is the retention strategy, I think. It's good pizza. Not five-days-a-week good, but good.'

The mandate's architects remain confident. CEO Bram Callahan, who announced the policy from his home in Jackson Hole, says the office is where 'serendipity happens' — spontaneous encounters that spark innovation. Employees agree serendipity is occurring. 'I ran into my old manager by the coffee machine, total chance,' said product designer Ana Reyes. 'Turns out she's leaving too. She introduced me to her new company's hiring manager right there. You can't schedule that. That's the magic of in-person.'

Middle managers describe an increasingly elaborate theater. 'My job now is walking the floor looking at screens,' said one engineering manager. 'Everyone has two windows open: work, and the other thing. The second they hear my footsteps, boom, work window. The whole floor sounds like a tap-dancing recital of alt-tab. I don't even care anymore. I have my own second window.'

The company's internal data tells a nuanced story. Office attendance is at 96%, exceeding targets. Meeting volume is up. Actual code shipped has declined 12%, which leadership attributes to 'the natural friction of re-onboarding to excellence.' Exit interviews, meanwhile, have become so frequent that HR now conducts them in groups, a format one departing employee described as 'ironically, the best team-building event I attended here.'

Real estate analysts note that Meridian signed a ten-year lease on its 14-story headquarters in 2022, a fact Callahan insists is 'completely unrelated' to the mandate, the new 'anchor days,' the mandatory culture summits, or the badge-swipe leaderboard published every Friday with the subject line 'Presence Report.'

Asked whether the company had considered that flexibility itself might be the retention tool, Harmon was thoughtful. 'We did an anonymous survey,' she said. '89% of employees said flexibility was their top priority. But here's the thing about surveys — they capture what people think they want.' She gestured at the office behind her, where three employees were quietly interviewing at other companies with their cameras off. 'Look at that energy. You can't get that at home.'

At press time, Meridian announced its next initiative to boost morale: a mandatory in-person town hall on the importance of trust, attendance to be tracked.
